## CALORIE NEEDS BY TRIMESTER (AND WEEK-BY-WEEK CHANGES)

Despite the old saying "eat for two," pregnant individuals should not simply double their food intake. Calorie needs increase only modestly and primarily in the later trimesters.

### First Trimester (Weeks 1–12): No General Increase in Calories Needed

During the first 12 weeks, you should eat roughly the same number of calories as you did pre-pregnancy. Focus on nutrient-dense foods, since early development (e.g., neural tube formation) requires quality nutrients like folate, even if total calories don't rise. If you experience nausea or vomiting in early pregnancy, aim for small frequent meals with healthy foods to meet nutrient needs.

**Nutritional Focus:**
- Folic acid (600 mcg) - critical for neural tube development
- Iron (27 mg) - for increased blood volume
- Calcium (1,000 mg) - foundation for fetal bones and teeth
- Vitamin D (600 IU) - works with calcium for bone health
- Vitamin B6 (1.9 mg) - may help alleviate morning sickness

**Meal Frequency:** 3 meals + 1–2 snacks

**Weight Gain Target:** 1–5 lbs total for first trimester

### Second Trimester (Weeks 13–26): Increase Intake by ~340 Extra Calories Per Day

Starting in the 2nd trimester, an additional ~340 calories per day is recommended to support the baby's rapid growth. For reference, 340 calories is about the amount in a cup of yogurt with fruit and a handful of nuts. These extra calories should come from nutritious sources (lean proteins, whole grains, fruits/vegetables, dairy or fortified alternatives) to supply needed vitamins and minerals along with energy.

**Week-by-Week Breakdown:**
- **Weeks 13–15:** Begin adding approximately 300–320 calories daily
- **Weeks 16–20:** Increase to 330–340 calories daily
- **Weeks 21–26:** Maintain 340 calories daily

**Nutritional Focus:**
- Iron (27 mg) - critical as blood volume expands
- Folic acid (600 mcg) - continue throughout pregnancy
- DHA omega-3s (200+ mg) - fetal brain development
- Iodine (220 mcg) - thyroid and brain development
- Choline (450 mg) - neural tube and brain development
- Calcium (1,000 mg) - beginning of major fetal bone development

**Meal Frequency:** 3 meals + 2 snacks

**Weight Gain Target:** Approximately 1 lb per week (10–14 lbs for weeks 13–26)

**Sample Daily Meal Plan (2,440 kcal total):**
- Breakfast: Oatmeal (1 cup) + berries + milk (350 cal)
- Snack: Apple + almond butter (150 cal)
- Lunch: 5 oz lean meat + sweet potato + broccoli (450 cal)
- Snack: Hummus + vegetables (100 cal)
- Dinner: 4 oz salmon + quinoa + salad (500 cal)
- Additional snack/beverage: 290 cal

### Third Trimester (Weeks 27–40): Increase Intake by ~450 Extra Calories Per Day

In the final trimester, the baby is growing the fastest and you need roughly 450 additional calories per day above your pre-pregnancy intake. This could be met, for example, by an extra healthy snack or small meal (e.g., a turkey sandwich and a glass of milk). Women carrying twins need even more—about 600 extra calories per day in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters (for triplets, around 900 extra).

**Week-by-Week Breakdown:**
- **Weeks 27–30:** Gradually increase to 400–420 calories daily
- **Weeks 31–35:** Maintain 420–450 calories daily
- **Weeks 36–40:** Maintain 450+ calories daily (adjust based on activity and hunger)

**Nutritional Focus:**
- Iron (27 mg) - preventing anemia in final weeks
- Calcium (1,000 mg) - rapid fetal bone development
- Zinc (11 mg) - immune system and cell growth
- Omega-3s (1.4 g) - continued brain development
- Vitamin C (85 mg) - collagen formation and iron absorption
- Potassium (2,600–4,700 mg) - blood pressure regulation

**Meal Frequency:** 5–6 smaller meals/snacks (to manage heartburn)

**Weight Gain Target:** Approximately 1 lb per week (13–14 lbs for weeks 27–40)

**Sample Daily Meal Plan (2,550 kcal total):**
- Early breakfast (6am): Toast + cheese (150 cal)
- Breakfast (8am): Scrambled eggs (2) + whole grain toast + fruit (350 cal)
- Snack (11am): Yogurt + granola (150 cal)
- Lunch (1pm): 5 oz turkey + vegetables + rice (450 cal)
- Snack (4pm): Nuts + fruit (100 cal)
- Dinner (6pm): 4 oz baked chicken + sweet potato + green beans (400 cal)
- Evening beverage: 50 cal (herbal tea, milk, etc.)

### Why Calories Increase

In later pregnancy, your body is not only nourishing a larger fetus but also supporting the growth of the placenta, amniotic fluid, and increased maternal tissues (like a larger uterus and higher blood volume). This results in higher energy (calorie) requirements. However, needs vary by individual—always monitor your weight gain and discuss with your healthcare provider to ensure you're on track.

## WEIGHT GAIN AND CALORIE ADJUSTMENTS

Total pregnancy weight gain recommendations depend on pre-pregnancy BMI:

| Pre-Pregnancy BMI Category | Recommended Total Weight Gain | First Trimester | Per Week (2nd–3rd Tri) |
|------|------|------|------|
| Underweight (BMI < 18.5) | 28–40 lbs | 1–5 lbs | 1–1.3 lbs |
| Normal Weight (BMI 18.5–24.9) | 25–35 lbs | 1–5 lbs | 0.8–1 lb |
| Overweight (BMI 25–29.9) | 15–25 lbs | 1–3 lbs | 0.5–0.7 lb |
| Obese (BMI ≥ 30) | 11–20 lbs | 0.5–2 lbs | 0.4–0.6 lb |

### Calorie Adjustments by Pre-Pregnancy Weight Status

**If you started underweight (BMI < 18.5):**
You have a higher target weight gain (up to 40 lbs). You might need slightly more calories than the standard additions to accomplish this. In practice, ensure you're eating the extra 340/450 kcal, and possibly more if you're not gaining enough. Nutrient-rich, calorie-dense snacks (like nuts, peanut butter, smoothies) can help. Always check with your provider before significantly increasing calories—they will monitor that weight gain is on track.

**If you started overweight (BMI 25–29.9) or obese (BMI ≥ 30):**
Your target weight gain is lower (15–25 lbs if overweight; 11–20 lbs if obese). This means you may not need all of the "standard" extra calories. Some women in this category might only need, say, an extra 200–300 kcal in 2nd tri and 300–400 kcal in 3rd, or sometimes no deliberate increase at all, especially if they have abundant energy stores. The focus should be on healthy food choices and possibly a modest calorie increase—enough to nourish the baby but avoid excessive maternal weight gain. It's a delicate balance: you should not attempt to lose weight during pregnancy, but rather slow the rate of gain. Work with your healthcare provider; they may have you meet with a dietitian to craft a plan. Regular physical activity (with doctor's approval) can also help manage weight while still supporting the pregnancy.

### Adjusting for Current Weight Gain Trajectory

Besides initial BMI, adjust intake based on how your weight gain is progressing:

- **If weight gain is below recommendations:** Increase calories (and nutrient intake) a bit to catch up. This might happen if you had severe morning sickness or appetite loss earlier. Adding an extra snack or two daily can help.

- **If weight gain is above recommendations (gaining too fast):** You may need to moderate your calorie intake and focus on lower-sugar, higher-protein foods. This doesn't mean dieting strictly, but maybe your "extra" calories were too high—try cutting out unnecessary sweets or second helpings of carbs/fats while still meeting protein and micronutrient needs. Never drastically cut calories in pregnancy; instead, make small adjustments and discuss with your provider.

Use pregnancy weight gain tracking tools (for example, the Institute of Medicine (IOM) gestational weight gain charts, which many doctors have) to see if you're on track. If not, tweak your intake accordingly as early as possible.

## MACRONUTRIENT REQUIREMENTS (PROTEIN, CARBS, FATS) IN PREGNANCY

Meeting macronutrient needs is essential, as these provide energy and building blocks for both maternal and fetal tissues. Pregnancy is a time to focus on quality: choose lean proteins, healthy fats, and complex carbohydrates to meet increased needs.

### Protein

Protein needs are elevated in pregnancy to support growth of fetal tissue (baby's developing organs, muscles), the placenta, and maternal tissues (like your increased blood supply and breast tissue). The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for protein in pregnancy is about **71 grams per day**, which is roughly 1.1 grams per kilogram of body weight. In practical terms, this means aiming for a **minimum of 60–75 grams of protein daily**.

**Protein Distribution Throughout the Day:**

Ensure you include some protein at each meal to help meet this goal:
- Breakfast: 15–20 g (2–3 eggs, yogurt, or meat)
- Snack: 5–10 g (nuts, cheese, yogurt)
- Lunch: 20–25 g (4–5 oz lean meat, fish, or legumes)
- Snack: 5–10 g (nuts, cheese, hummus)
- Dinner: 20–25 g (4–5 oz lean meat, fish, or legumes)

**Protein should make up roughly 20%–25% of your calories during pregnancy.**

**High-Quality Protein Sources:**
- Lean meats (beef, pork, lamb)
- Poultry (chicken, turkey—remove skin for lower fat)
- Fish (especially low-mercury varieties like salmon, tilapia)
- Eggs (excellent source with choline)
- Dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese)
- Legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas)
- Tofu and tempeh
- Nuts and seeds
- Whole grains

**Special Note:** If you're carrying multiples like twins, protein needs may be even higher (up to 100+ g per day)—your doctor or a dietitian can provide specific targets.

### Carbohydrates

Carbs are the body's main fuel and also crucial for fetal energy needs (especially for the developing brain, which uses glucose). Pregnant individuals should eat at least **175 grams of carbohydrates per day** to ensure adequate glucose supply for the baby (this is up from 130 g/day for non-pregnant women).

In terms of total calorie distribution, about **45%–65% of your calories should come from carbohydrates**.

**Prioritize:**
- Complex carbohydrates (whole grains, legumes, starchy vegetables)
- Natural sugars (fruits)
- High-fiber options

**Include ample fiber:** Aim for ~28 g of dietary fiber per day (general guideline of 14 g per 1,000 kcal) to support healthy digestion. A high-fiber diet (fruits, vegetables, whole grains, nuts) helps prevent the constipation common in pregnancy.

**Carbohydrate Sources:**
- Whole grains (oats, brown rice, whole wheat bread)
- Starchy vegetables (sweet potatoes, regular potatoes, peas, corn)
- Legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas)
- Fruits (berries, apples, bananas, oranges)
- Vegetables (broccoli, spinach, carrots, bell peppers)

**If you are diagnosed with gestational diabetes (GD):** You may need to moderate carbohydrate intake to about 40%–50% of calories and distribute carbs evenly in small meals to keep blood sugar stable. Always follow your healthcare provider's specific advice if you have GD.

### Fats

Healthy fats are important for nutrient absorption and fetal development (especially for the baby's brain and nervous system). During pregnancy, about **25%–35% of your daily calories should come from fat**—similar to general adult recommendations, but make sure these are good fats:

**Emphasize unsaturated fats:**
- Olive oil
- Avocado
- Nuts and seeds
- Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, trout)
- Plant-based oils

**Omega-3 Fatty Acids:**
In particular, omega-3 fatty acids are crucial for fetal brain and eye development. Pregnant women need about **1.4 g/day of omega-3 fats**. This includes alpha-linolenic acid (ALA) from plant sources and importantly DHA (an omega-3 found in fish). Experts recommend at least **200 mg of DHA per day during pregnancy**—you can get this by eating **1–2 servings of low-mercury fatty fish per week** (such as salmon, sardines) or via a prenatal supplement with DHA.

**Limit saturated fats** to <10% of daily calories (this is about <22 g sat fat on a 2,000 kcal diet). High intakes of saturated fat (found in butter, high-fat meats, full-fat dairy, fried foods) can impact heart health. Choose leaner cuts of meat and low-fat dairy when possible.

**Avoid trans fats** as much as possible (many processed and fried foods contain hydrogenated oils). Trans fats are known to be unhealthy and should be minimized in all diets, including during pregnancy. Check ingredient labels for "partially hydrogenated oils" and avoid those products.

### Cholesterol

There is no specific RDA for cholesterol, as your body can make what it needs. However, it's wise to consume cholesterol-rich foods (egg yolks, organ meats, shellfish) in moderation and instead focus on unsaturated fats. Past guidelines suggested a limit of 300 mg of dietary cholesterol per day, but current thinking is to simply keep intake moderate and emphasize healthy fats to maintain good cholesterol levels.

### Water and Hydration

Hydration needs increase in pregnancy. You should drink plenty of fluids—about **8 to 12 cups of water per day** is a common recommendation. Proper hydration supports the increased blood volume and amniotic fluid. Try to sip water throughout the day, not just when thirsty.

Other fluids like milk, herbal teas, soups, and water-rich fruits also contribute to hydration. (Limit sugary drinks and avoid alcohol entirely—see below.)

## KEY MICRONUTRIENT NEEDS (VITAMINS AND MINERALS)

Pregnancy significantly increases the need for certain vitamins and minerals. A daily prenatal vitamin is often recommended to help meet these requirements, but it's still important to eat a balanced diet rich in micronutrients. Below are the key vitamins and minerals needed in pregnancy, along with their recommended daily amounts and dietary sources.

### Folic Acid (Vitamin B9)

**RDA: 600 µg per day**

Adequate folate is critical before conception and in early pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects in the baby's brain and spine. Because it's hard to get enough from food alone, women should take a prenatal vitamin supplying at least 400 µg of folic acid starting at least 1 month before pregnancy and through the first trimester.

**Good food sources:**
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale, romaine)
- Beans and lentils
- Citrus fruits and juices
- Fortified grains and cereals
- Asparagus
- Broccoli

**Note:** Many prenatal vitamins provide 600–800 µg to cover this need.

### Iron

**RDA: 27 mg per day** (versus 18 mg for nonpregnant women)

Iron is essential to make extra red blood cells for you and the baby; it prevents anemia and supports fetal growth. High-iron foods include red meat, poultry, fish, beans, lentils, spinach, and fortified cereals. Because iron needs jump in pregnancy, many women rely on their prenatal vitamin for additional iron.

**Important Tips:**
- **Vitamin C aids iron absorption:** Eat iron-rich foods with a source of vitamin C (like citrus fruits or tomatoes)
- **Avoid competing nutrients:** Do not take iron supplements at the same time as calcium (they compete for absorption)
- **If diagnosed with iron-deficiency anemia:** Your doctor may recommend a higher iron dose (e.g., 60–100 mg/day supplemental iron)

**High-iron food sources:**
- Red meat (beef, lamb)
- Poultry (chicken, turkey)
- Fish (salmon, sardines)
- Beans and lentils
- Spinach and dark leafy greens
- Fortified cereals
- Eggs (especially the yolk)

### Calcium

**RDA: 1,000 mg per day** for pregnant adults (19–50 years old)

Teens (≤18) need even more (1,300 mg). Surprisingly, the calcium requirement is the same as for non-pregnant women, but it's critical to meet it because calcium is needed for building the baby's bones and teeth, especially in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters. **If you don't get enough calcium, the baby will draw it from your bones.**

**Calcium sources:**
- Dairy products (milk, yogurt, cheese)
- Fortified plant milks (almond, soy, oat)
- Calcium-set tofu
- Canned fish with bones (sardines, salmon)
- Leafy greens (collard greens, bok choy)
- Sesame seeds

**Important Note:** Do not take a calcium supplement at the exact same time as your prenatal or iron supplement—calcium can hinder iron absorption. Space them out by at least 2 hours if possible.

A prenatal vitamin usually contains some calcium, but often not the full 1,000 mg, so use diet (and possibly a calcium supplement if you don't consume dairy) to reach this target.

### Vitamin D

**RDA: 600 IU per day** (15 µg)

This is the same as for other adults. Vitamin D works with calcium for bone health and supports the immune system. Our bodies make vitamin D from sunlight, but many people require dietary sources or supplements, especially in pregnancy.

**Food sources:**
- Fortified milk or orange juice
- Fatty fish (like salmon, mackerel, herring)
- Egg yolks
- Fortified cereals

**Note:** Many prenatal vitamins provide 400–600 IU; some doctors may advise an extra vitamin D supplement if your levels are low. Adequate vitamin D in pregnancy helps ensure proper fetal bone development and may reduce the risk of complications.

### Vitamin A

**RDA: About 770 µg RAE per day**

This is equivalent to 770 µg retinol activity equivalents, or roughly 2,565 IU if from retinol. Vitamin A is important for embryonic growth (especially for the development of the eyes, heart, and immune system).

**CAUTION: More is not better with vitamin A.** Very high doses (especially from retinol supplements or medications like isotretinoin) can cause birth defects. It's best to get vitamin A from a balanced diet:

**Safe sources:**
- Orange and dark-green produce (sweet potatoes, carrots, spinach, kale, mango)—these provide beta-carotene, a safe vitamin A precursor
- Animal sources (dairy, eggs)—provide retinol; eat in moderation

**AVOID:** Taking extra high-dose vitamin A supplements beyond your prenatal pill. Stick with the RDA and do not exceed the upper limit (~3,000 µg RAE or 10,000 IU of retinol).

### Vitamin C

**RDA: 85 mg per day** (up from 75 mg when not pregnant)

Vitamin C supports the immune system, helps form collagen (for skin, blood vessels, etc.), and increases iron absorption.

**Excellent sources:**
- Citrus fruits (orange, grapefruit, lemon)
- Berries (strawberries, blueberries)
- Kiwis
- Tomatoes
- Bell peppers (red, yellow, green)
- Broccoli
- Papaya

**Note:** Most prenatals have some vitamin C included.

### Vitamin E

**RDA: 15 mg per day**

This is the same as for non-pregnant adults. Vitamin E is an antioxidant important for immune function and cellular signaling.

**Sources:**
- Nuts (almonds, peanuts)
- Seeds (sunflower seeds, pumpkin seeds)
- Vegetable oils (wheat germ oil, sunflower oil)
- Leafy greens

**Note:** Typically, you can meet this through diet; prenatal vitamins also often include vitamin E. Avoid mega-doses of vitamin E supplements, as high amounts can be harmful—stick to the recommended amount.

### Vitamin K

**RDA: 90 µg per day**

This is unchanged from non-pregnant needs. Vitamin K is needed for normal blood clotting and bone health. It's found in leafy greens (spinach, kale, broccoli) and also produced by gut bacteria.

**Sources:**
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale, collard greens)
- Broccoli
- Cabbage
- Brussels sprouts

**Note:** Deficiency is rare in adults, but you should eat green vegetables regularly to get this nutrient.

### B Vitamins (B1, B2, B3, B6, B12)

All B vitamins are involved in energy metabolism and fetal development.

**Thiamin (B1):**
- RDA: ~1.4 mg/day in pregnancy (up from 1.1 mg)
- Found in: whole grains, pork, legumes, nuts, seeds

**Riboflavin (B2):**
- RDA: 1.4 mg/day (up from 1.1 mg)
- Found in: dairy, eggs, lean meats, green vegetables

**Niacin (B3):**
- RDA: 18 mg NE/day (niacin equivalents) (up from 14 mg)
- Found in: meats, fish, whole grains, legumes, mushrooms
- Your prenatal vitamin likely covers thiamin, riboflavin, niacin in these amounts

**Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine):**
- RDA: 1.9 mg/day (up from 1.3 mg)
- B6 helps form red blood cells and may help with nausea
- Found in: chicken, fish, potatoes, bananas, chickpeas
- Most prenatals contain B6; if you have severe morning sickness, your doctor might even recommend additional B6 supplementation (25–50 mg) to help alleviate nausea

**Vitamin B12:**
- RDA: 2.6 µg/day (slightly up from 2.4 µg)
- B12 is crucial for neurodevelopment and works with folate
- Found in: animal foods (meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy)
- **Vegetarians and vegans:** Should ensure they get enough B12 through fortified foods or supplements, as B12 is primarily in animal products. Prenatal vitamins usually provide B12.

### Choline

**Adequate Intake (AI): 450 mg per day**

Choline is a nutrient essential for fetal brain development (especially for neural tube formation and memory centers in the brain) and helps prevent certain birth defects. Many people aren't aware of choline needs.

**Sources:**
- Egg yolks (one yolk ~147 mg)
- Meat, poultry, fish
- Dairy
- Nuts and seeds
- Beans and legumes

**Important Note:** Many prenatal vitamins do not include much choline, so dietary sources are important or you may need a separate choline supplement. Aim for ~450 mg daily from food plus supplements. (For reference, non-pregnant women need 425 mg, and breastfeeding women need 550 mg of choline per day.)

### Iodine

**RDA: 220 µg per day**

Iodine is critical for making thyroid hormones, which regulate your metabolism and are vital for the baby's brain development. Iodine needs increase in pregnancy (from 150 µg pre-pregnancy to 220 µg).

**Sources:**
- Iodized salt (check that your table salt is iodized)
- Dairy products
- Seafood
- Seaweed
- Eggs

**Note:** Most prenatal vitamins now include 150 mcg of iodine—combined with a normal diet this helps reach the 220 µg target. Getting enough iodine can help prevent developmental delays and congenital hypothyroidism in the baby.

### Magnesium

**RDA: ~350–360 mg per day** for adult pregnant women (exact recommendation depends on age: 350 mg/day for ages 19–30; 360 mg for 31–50)

Magnesium supports hundreds of biochemical reactions, including muscle and nerve function, and it contributes to bone structure.

**Sources:**
- Nuts (almonds, peanuts)
- Seeds (pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds)
- Whole grains
- Legumes
- Leafy greens
- Peas
- Chocolate (dark)

**Note:** Many prenatals include some magnesium, but usually not the full amount due to pill size limits (magnesium is bulky). Ensuring a diet with magnesium-rich foods is important; it may also help with leg cramps and constipation.

### Potassium

**AI: ≈2,900–3,400 mg per day**

The general recommendation for adult women is around 2,600 mg, and pregnancy slightly increases needs. However, many health guidelines (like Dietary Guidelines for Americans) suggest aiming for **4,700 mg as an optimal intake** for adults to support heart and muscle function and to balance sodium. Potassium helps maintain healthy blood pressure and fluid balance.

**Rich sources:**
- Bananas
- Oranges and citrus fruits
- Potatoes and sweet potatoes
- Tomato sauce
- Beans and legumes
- Spinach and leafy greens
- Avocado
- Cantaloupe

**Important Note:** Most people fall short on potassium, so consciously include these foods. There's no prenatal supplement for potassium due to its high amount needed—food is the main source.

### Zinc

**RDA: 11 mg per day** (up from 8 mg)

Zinc is crucial for cell growth and a healthy immune system.

**Sources:**
- Meat (beef, pork)
- Shellfish
- Beans and legumes
- Nuts
- Whole grains
- Seeds
- Cheese

**Note:** Most prenatal vitamins provide around 11–15 mg of zinc.

### Copper

**RDA: 1.0 mg per day** (up slightly from 0.9 mg)

Copper works with iron to form red blood cells and supports fetal heart and immune development.

**Sources:**
- Nuts (cashews, almonds)
- Seeds
- Whole grains
- Organ meats
- Cocoa/dark chocolate
- Shellfish
- Legumes

**Note:** Prenatal vitamins often have about 1 mg of copper.

### Selenium

**RDA: 60 µg per day** (vs 55 µg for non-pregnant)

Selenium supports thyroid function and has antioxidant roles.

**Sources:**
- Brazil nuts (CAUTION: Brazil nuts are extremely rich in selenium, so just 1–2 nuts occasionally is plenty)
- Seafood (fish, shrimp)
- Meats
- Grains
- Dairy

**Important:** Most prenatals have ~50–70 µg. Do not exceed the upper limit (400 µg) as too much selenium can be toxic.

### Manganese

**RDA: 2.0 mg per day** (up from 1.8 mg)

Manganese helps form bone and cartilage.

**Sources:**
- Whole grains
- Nuts
- Leafy vegetables
- Tea
- Legumes
- Pineapple

**Note:** Prenatal vitamins may include a small amount (2 mg).

### Chromium

**RDA: 30 µg per day**

Chromium is needed for macronutrient metabolism and blood sugar regulation. Requirements are slightly higher in pregnancy.

**Sources:**
- Whole grains
- Meats
- Broccoli
- Fruit
- Beer (not recommended during pregnancy)

**Note:** Often included in small amounts in prenatal supplements.

## PRENATAL VITAMINS

Taking a daily **prenatal vitamin is highly recommended** because it ensures you get adequate folic acid, iron, iodine, and other micronutrients that can be hard to obtain in ideal amounts from diet alone.

**Important Points:**
- A pill is **supplemental**—you should still eat a balanced diet
- Nutrients like fiber, protein, and the diverse array of phytonutrients in fruits/veggies only come from food
- Use the prenatal vitamin as nutritional "insurance"
- Never take more than the directed dose
- If your healthcare provider finds you need extra of a specific nutrient (like iron or calcium), they will recommend an additional supplement separately

**What to Look For in a Prenatal Vitamin:**
- At least 400 µg folic acid (600–800 µg is common)
- 27 mg iron
- 1,000 mg calcium (or plan to get remainder from diet)
- 600 IU vitamin D
- 200+ mg DHA (omega-3)
- 150 mcg iodine
- 200 mcg selenium
- Should ideally include some choline (though many don't provide the full 450 mg)

**Taking Your Prenatal:**
- Take with food to improve absorption and reduce nausea
- Take iron separate from calcium (at least 2 hours apart)
- If you experience constipation, speak with your doctor about alternatives
- Continue prenatal vitamins through breastfeeding if nursing

## LIMITS AND CAUTIONS: SUGAR, SODIUM, CAFFEINE, AND MORE

In addition to getting enough of the healthy nutrients above, it's important to limit certain ingredients for the safety of your pregnancy and to maintain overall health.

### Added Sugars

**Limit "empty" calories from added sugars.** The general guideline for everyone (including pregnant women) is to consume **<10% of your daily calories from added sugars**. That equates to **at most ~50 grams of added sugar per day** on a 2,000 calorie diet (about 12 teaspoons of sugar).

High sugar intake can contribute to excessive weight gain and increased risk of gestational diabetes. Try to satisfy your sweet tooth with fresh fruits (which provide natural sugars along with fiber and vitamins) rather than candies, desserts, or sugary drinks.

**Watch for added sugars in:**
- Sodas and sugary beverages
- Candies and sweets
- Pastries and donuts
- Sugary cereals
- Flavored yogurts
- Desserts and cakes
- Sweetened juices
- Chocolate drinks
- Sweetened condensed milk

**If you have diabetes or gestational diabetes:** You'll need to be even more stringent—follow your doctor's or dietitian's advice, which often includes spreading out carbohydrate and sugar intake and focusing on high-fiber carbs.

### Sodium (Salt)

Pregnant women should generally consume **no more than about 2,300 mg of sodium per day** (which is roughly one teaspoon of table salt)—this is the same limit recommended for the general population to support healthy blood pressure.

In fact, many health professionals advise aiming for **1,500–2,300 mg/day** if possible, since most people exceed this. There is no need to eat extra salt when pregnant; your requirement doesn't increase.

**Avoid very high salt diets:**
- Fast foods
- Salty snacks (chips, pretzels, crackers)
- Processed foods
- Canned soups (unless low-sodium)

**In some cases** (e.g., if you have high blood pressure or risk of preeclampsia), your doctor might advise moderate salt restriction. However, severe sodium restriction isn't recommended for most pregnant women—just keep sodium in the normal recommended range.

**Tip:** Eating plenty of potassium-rich foods (fruits, vegetables) as mentioned earlier also helps balance sodium's effect on blood pressure.

### Caffeine

**It's safe to have some caffeine during pregnancy, but limit your intake to <200 mg of caffeine per day.** This is about the amount in one 12-ounce cup of coffee.

High caffeine consumption has been linked to increased risk of miscarriage and low birth weight, so moderation is key.

**Caffeine Content Reference:**
- 8 oz brewed coffee: ~95 mg
- 12 oz coffee: ~190 mg
- 8 oz black tea: ~47 mg
- 8 oz green tea: ~30 mg
- 12 oz cola: ~35 mg
- Energy drinks: 50–300+ mg per serving
- Dark chocolate (1 oz): ~5–10 mg

**Important:** Also remember chocolate and certain energy drinks contain caffeine. Stay under 200 mg total.

**Expert Recommendation:** If possible, some experts suggest keeping it around **100 mg or less especially in the first trimester** as a precaution.

**Safe alternatives:**
- Decaf coffee and tea
- Caffeine-free herbal teas (pregnancy-safe varieties)
- Water
- Milk
- Fresh fruit juice (pasteurized)
- Hot chocolate (minimal caffeine)

### Alcohol

**No safe level of alcohol has been established in pregnancy—it is recommended to avoid alcohol entirely while pregnant.**

Drinking alcohol (beer, wine, liquor, or any alcoholic beverage) can cause serious birth defects and developmental disorders (fetal alcohol spectrum disorders). The baby's brain and organs are developing throughout pregnancy, so abstaining from alcohol for the whole 9 months is the safest choice.

**Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD) can include:**
- Developmental delays
- Intellectual disabilities
- Behavioral problems
- Heart defects
- Facial abnormalities
- Low birth weight
- Miscarriage and stillbirth

### Fish and Mercury

Fish can be an excellent source of protein and omega-3s (especially DHA as noted above), but certain fish are high in mercury, which can harm a baby's developing nervous system.

**Pregnant women should AVOID high-mercury fish:**
- Shark
- Swordfish
- King mackerel
- Tilefish
- Bigeye tuna
- Marlin
- Orange roughy

**Limit white (albacore) tuna** to ~6 oz per week (higher mercury than light tuna).

**Instead, CHOOSE low-mercury options (8–12 oz per week total):**
- Salmon
- Sardines
- Trout (freshwater)
- Tilapia
- Shrimp
- Pollock
- Light canned tuna
- Cod
- Catfish
- Sole
- Whitefish
- Black sea bass
- Herring
- Anchovies

**Aim for 8–12 oz per week of low-mercury fish to get benefits of omega-3s.** If you don't eat fish, consider a DHA supplement (200–300 mg per day).

## COMPREHENSIVE FOODS TO AVOID DURING PREGNANCY

### 1. Raw or Undercooked Meats & Poultry

**Foods to Avoid:**
- Raw or undercooked beef, pork, lamb, and veal
- Raw or undercooked poultry (chicken, turkey, duck)
- Undercooked burgers and ground meat
- Steak tartare or carpaccio
- Raw or rare meat patties
- Undercooked minced meat
- Any meat with pink or raw areas in the center

**Why:**
Raw and undercooked meats can harbor harmful bacteria including:
- **Salmonella** - causes food poisoning with severe symptoms in pregnant women
- **E. coli** - can lead to severe infection and complications
- **Listeria** - can cross the placenta and cause miscarriage, stillbirth, or serious newborn infection
- **Toxoplasmosis parasites** - can cause birth defects and developmental issues

**Safe Alternative:**
Cook all meat thoroughly to an internal temperature of **165°F (74°C)**. Use a meat thermometer to ensure doneness.

### 2. Raw or Undercooked Seafood & Fish

**Raw Seafood to Avoid:**
- Raw fish (sushi, sashimi, ceviche)
- Raw oysters, clams, and mussels
- Raw scallops
- Raw lobster
- Raw shrimp
- Undercooked fish
- Any uncooked shellfish

**Why:**
Raw seafood can contain:
- **Listeria bacteria** - causes listeriosis, which is 10 times more likely in pregnant women (with 20% pregnancy loss rate)
- **Salmonella** - causes severe foodborne illness
- **Parasites** - can harm fetal development
- **Vibrio bacteria** - causes acute gastroenteritis
- **Hepatitis A and Norovirus** - causes viral infections

**Safe Alternative:**
Eat only fully cooked fish and shellfish. Cooked seafood should reach an internal temperature of **145°F (63°C)**.

### 3. Smoked & Cured Fish

**Foods to Avoid (unless heated until steaming hot):**
- Smoked salmon (lox)
- Nova-style fish
- Kippered fish
- Jerky-style fish
- Cold-smoked seafood
- Cured fish

**Why:**
Unless heated until steaming hot, these products can contain **Listeria monocytogenes**, which:
- Causes severe infections in pregnancy
- Can cross the placenta
- Leads to miscarriage, stillbirth, or serious newborn infections
- Pregnant women are 10 times more susceptible than general population

**Safe Alternative:**
Heat smoked or cured fish until steaming hot (internal temperature 165°F/74°C) before consuming.

### 4. Deli Meats & Processed Meats

**Meats to Avoid or Use Caution:**
- Hot dogs and frankfurters (unless heated until steaming)
- Lunch meats and cold cuts
- Pepperoni
- Salami
- Prosciutto
- Mortadella
- Bologna
- Corned beef
- Beef jerky (uncooked)
- Dry-cured sausages
- Canned meats (pâté, deviled ham)
- Ready-to-eat meat products
- Chorizo (uncooked)

**Why:**
**Deli and processed meats are the leading cause of listeriosis in the U.S.** for the past five years. They can harbor:
- **Listeria** from processing equipment, slicers, and contaminated surfaces
- High levels of sodium and nitrates
- Parasites that cause toxoplasmosis

**Risk Facts:**
- Pregnant women are **10 times more likely to get listeria**
- About **20% of pregnant women with listeria infection lose their baby**

**Safe Alternative:**
- Cook deli meats until steaming hot (165°F/74°C) before eating
- Choose freshly cooked meats from the deli counter rather than pre-packaged cold cuts
- Look for uncured, low-sodium options if available

### 5. Liver & Organ Meats

**Organ Meats to Avoid:**
- Beef liver and liver products
- Chicken liver
- Pâté (all types, including vegetarian)
- Liver sausage
- Foie gras
- Liverwurst
- Any game bird organs
- Kidney
- Tripe

**Why:**
Organ meats contain **extremely high levels of vitamin A (retinol)**, which:
- Can cause birth defects in excess amounts
- Damages fetal development, especially in early pregnancy
- Increases risk of cleft palate and heart defects
- Can cause developmental delays

**Upper safe limit:** Do not exceed 3,000 μg RAE (10,000 IU) retinol daily

### 6. Game Meats

**Game Animals to Avoid:**
- Venison (deer)
- Elk
- Moose
- Duck
- Goose
- Partridge
- Pheasant
- Rabbit
- Wild boar

**Why:**
- May contain **lead shot** from ammunition
- Lead exposure during pregnancy causes neurological damage to the fetus
- Can accumulate in the baby's developing brain
- May contain parasites or toxoplasmosis
- Higher risk of contamination from hunting methods

**Safe Alternative:**
Stick to conventionally raised poultry and domesticated meats.

### 7. Raw or Undercooked Eggs

**Egg Products to Avoid:**
- Raw or lightly cooked eggs
- Runny or soft-boiled eggs
- Poached eggs with runny yolks
- Over-easy or sunny-side-up eggs (with runny yolk)
- Raw cookie dough and cake batter
- Homemade Caesar dressing (contains raw eggs)
- Homemade mayonnaise
- Homemade hollandaise sauce
- Homemade aioli
- Homemade egg nog
- Homemade ice cream
- Some homemade salad dressings
- Homemade cake icing/frosting
- Eggs Benedict (with runny yolk)
- Tiramisu (contains raw eggs)
- Mousse (if made with raw eggs)
- Lightly scrambled eggs

**Why:**
Raw eggs can contain **Salmonella bacteria**, which:
- Causes severe food poisoning
- Increases risk of miscarriage
- Can lead to preterm labor
- Causes serious infection in newborns

**Safe Alternative:**
- Cook eggs until both whites and yolks are firm (hard-boiled, well-cooked scrambled, over-hard)
- Use pasteurized eggs in recipes that require raw eggs
- Buy commercial products with pasteurized eggs (labeled on package)

### 8. Unpasteurized & Soft Cheeses

**Soft Cheeses to AVOID (unless pasteurized):**
- Brie
- Camembert
- Chèvre (goat cheese, unless cooked)
- Roquefort
- Feta
- Gorgonzola
- Danish blue
- Stilton
- Blue cheese (unless cooked hot)
- Queso fresco
- Panela
- Queso blanco
- Cream cheese (if unpasteurized)
- Goat cheese with white rind
- Ricotta (if unpasteurized)
- Mozzarella (if unpasteurized)
- Burrata

**Why:**
Unpasteurized soft cheeses can contain:
- **Listeria monocytogenes** - causes listeriosis
- **E. coli** - causes severe infection
- Other harmful bacteria that cross the placenta

**Safe Cheeses:**
- **Hard cheeses** (pasteurized or unpasteurized):
  - Cheddar
  - Parmesan
  - Gruyère
  - Swiss cheese
  - Comté
  
- **Pasteurized soft cheeses**:
  - Pasteurized feta
  - Pasteurized brie (check label)
  - Pasteurized cream cheese
  - Cottage cheese (pasteurized)
  - Ricotta (if pasteurized)
  - Mozzarella (if pasteurized)
  - Halloumi

**Important:** **Always check the label** to confirm pasteurization. Soft cheeses can be safe if cooked until steaming hot (165°F/74°C).

### 9. Unpasteurized Milk & Dairy

**Products to Avoid:**
- Raw (unpasteurized) cow's milk
- Unpasteurized goat's milk
- Unpasteurized sheep's milk
- Unpasteurized cream
- Unpasteurized yogurt
- Unpasteurized kefir
- Any product labeled "raw"

**Why:**
Unpasteurized dairy can contain:
- **Listeria**
- **E. coli O157:H7**
- **Salmonella**
- **Brucella**
- Other harmful bacteria

**Safe Alternative:**
- Choose **pasteurized milk, yogurt, cream, and ice cream**
- Check all dairy labels for "pasteurized"
- Pasteurization kills harmful bacteria while retaining nutrients

### 10. Unpasteurized Juices & Ciders

**Juices to Avoid:**
- Fresh-pressed apple cider
- Fresh-squeezed juice from juice bars
- Freshly made vegetable juices
- Any juice not marked pasteurized
- Homemade fruit or vegetable juices
- Unpasteurized juice concentrate

**Why:**
Unpasteurized juices can contain:
- **E. coli O157:H7**
- **Listeria**
- **Salmonella**
- **Cryptosporidium**

**Safe Alternative:**
- Pasteurized juices (labeled on container)
- Commercial juice brands
- Fresh whole fruits and vegetables (properly washed)
- Smoothies made with pasteurized ingredients

### 11. Raw Sprouts

**Sprouts to Avoid (Raw):**
- Alfalfa sprouts
- Mung bean sprouts
- Radish sprouts
- Broccoli sprouts (raw)
- Clover sprouts
- Any sprouted seeds consumed raw

**Why:**
Raw sprouts can harbor:
- **Salmonella** and **E. coli** in the seeds
- Bacteria that grow in warm, moist sprouting conditions
- Organisms difficult to wash away due to sprout structure

**Safe Alternative:**
- Cooked/heated sprouts
- Cook sprouts until soft
- Choose bean sprouts that are cooked
- Mature, cooked vegetables instead

### 12. Unwashed Produce

**Produce to Handle Carefully:**
- Pre-cut salads and vegetables
- Ready-to-eat salad mixes
- Unwashed lettuce and spinach
- Unwashed raw vegetables
- Unwashed berries
- Unwashed cantaloupe (especially important for listeria—second most contaminated food)

**Why:**
Can be contaminated with:
- **Listeria** (especially leafy greens and cantaloupe)
- **E. coli**
- **Salmonella**
- Parasites and pesticides

**Safe Alternative:**
- **Wash all produce thoroughly** under running water
- Wash hands before and after handling produce
- Use produce brush for hard vegetables
- Cook leafy greens when possible during pregnancy
- Choose cooked vegetables as your main vegetable source
- Avoid pre-cut produce; wash whole vegetables yourself

### 13. Herbal Teas & Supplements (Use Caution)

**Herbal Teas to AVOID (in high doses):**
- High-dose herbal weight loss teas
- Herbal remedies not studied for pregnancy
- High amounts of:
  - Sage tea (large amounts)
  - Rosemary tea (excessive)
  - Ginger supplements (high doses; small amounts safe)
  - Peppermint tea (large quantities)
  - Licorice root tea
  - Cohosh (black or blue)
  - Pennyroyal
  - Mugwort

**Safe Herbal Teas (in moderation):**
- Ginger tea (small amounts for nausea)
- Chamomile (moderate amounts)
- Rooibos
- Nettle leaf (pregnancy-specific)
- Red raspberry leaf (late pregnancy, with provider approval)
- Pregnancy-specific blends

**Important:**
- Always consult healthcare provider before herbal supplements
- Many herbs lack safety data for pregnancy
- "Natural" does not always mean safe

### 14. High-Dose Vitamin & Mineral Supplements

**Supplements to Avoid Without Doctor Approval:**
- High-dose vitamin A supplements (retinol above RDA)
- Vitamin A from fish oil (unless approved)
- High-dose vitamin E supplements
- Certain herbal supplements
- Uncontrolled megavitamins
- Weight loss supplements
- Detox supplements

**Why:**
- Some vitamins are toxic in excess during pregnancy
- Can cause birth defects
- May interfere with fetal development
- Excessive amounts unnecessary

**Safe Approach:**
- Take only the **prenatal vitamin recommended** by your healthcare provider
- Do not take additional supplements without approval
- Inform provider of all supplements you take

### 15. Artificial Sweeteners (Use With Caution)

**Sweeteners Generally Considered Safer:**
- Sucralose (Splenda) - considered safer
- Aspartame (Equal, NutraSweet) - FDA approved but use moderately
- Stevia (Truvia) - plant-based, generally considered safe

**Sweeteners to AVOID or Limit Significantly:**
- Saccharin - crosses placenta; some concern
- Cyclamate - banned in many countries
- Acesulfame-K (Ace-K) - limited safety data in pregnancy

**Why:**
- Limited safety data specifically for pregnancy
- Some concerns about fetal development effects
- May affect metabolism
- Individual sensitivity varies

**Safer Natural Alternatives:**
- Honey (pasteurized)
- Maple syrup
- Agave nectar
- Dates (whole or paste)
- Fresh fruits

**Note:**
Use minimal amounts of artificial sweeteners. Moderation is key. When possible, choose natural sources of sweetness.

### 16. Certain Food Additives & Processed Foods

**Additives to Minimize:**
- BPA (Bisphenol A) in plastics - avoid heating food in plastic
- MSG in excess
- Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils)
- Certain food colorings (red dye #3, some others)
- Nitrates and nitrites (in cured meats)
- Sulfites
- BHA and BHT (preservatives)

**Why:**
- Some linked to developmental issues
- May affect fetal growth
- Trans fats increase inflammation
- Long-term effects not fully studied in pregnancy

**Safe Approach:**
- Read food labels carefully
- Choose fresh, whole foods
- Avoid heavily processed items
- Choose products without added preservatives when possible

### 17. High-Fat & Ultra-Processed Foods

**Foods to Avoid or Limit:**
- Fast food meals
- Deep-fried foods
- High-fat baked goods
- Processed snacks
- Highly refined carbohydrates
- Foods high in trans fats
- Excessive butter and cream-based sauces

**Why:**
- Associated with metabolic issues in baby
- Increases infant obesity risk
- Excessive weight gain
- Reduced nutrient density
- May increase preeclampsia risk

## PERSONALIZING CALORIE INTAKE: FORMULA BASED ON WEIGHT, HEIGHT, AND WEEK

Every individual is different—factors like your pre-pregnancy weight, height, and activity level influence how many calories you specifically need. The goal is to eat enough to support a healthy rate of weight gain for your situation. Here's how you can estimate personalized needs:

### Calculate Baseline Caloric Needs (Pre-Pregnancy)

First, determine what your maintenance calories were before pregnancy. This can be estimated using a formula for Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) plus an activity factor:

**Mifflin-St Jeor Formula for BMR:**

\(BMR_{female} = 10 \times \text{weight(kg)} + 6.25 \times \text{height(cm)} - 5 \times \text{age(years)} - 161\)

This gives an estimate of how many calories you burn at rest.

**Example:** A 30-year-old woman who is 165 cm tall (5'5") and weighed 68 kg (~150 lbs) pre-pregnancy has:

\(10 \times 68 + 6.25 \times 165 - 5 \times 30 - 161 = 1,400 \text{ kcal/day BMR}\)

**Next, multiply BMR by an activity factor** to account for your typical daily activity/exercise:

- Sedentary (little exercise): × 1.2
- Light activity: × 1.3
- Moderate (exercise a few times a week): × 1.5
- Very active: × 1.7

If our example woman is moderately active, her pre-pregnancy maintenance need might be:

\(1,400 \times 1.5 = 2,100 \text{ kcal/day}\)

### Add Pregnancy Calorie Needs

Now add the extra calories for the current stage of pregnancy:

- **1st trimester (≤12 weeks):** add 0 (no extra calories yet)
- **2nd trimester (13–26 weeks):** add about +340 kcal/day
- **3rd trimester (27+ weeks):** add about +450 kcal/day

These are averages—you might start with +300 in early 2nd trimester and move toward +450 by late 3rd trimester.

**For our example (moderately active, maintenance ~2,100 kcal):**

- If she's 8 weeks pregnant (1st tri): target ~2,100 kcal (same as before)
- At 20 weeks (mid-2nd tri): target ~2,100 + 340 = 2,440 kcal/day
- At 35 weeks (3rd tri): target ~2,100 + 450 = 2,550 kcal/day

**These calories should come from nutritious foods as outlined above—quality matters for both mother and baby.**

### Example Formula for Weekly Calculation

An approximate formula for daily caloric needs at a given week of pregnancy:

\(\text{Calories needed per day} = (BMR \times \text{activity factor}) + \text{pregnancy\_increment}\)

Where:
\(\text{pregnancy\_increment} = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } w \leq 12 \\
340 & \text{if } 13 \leq w \leq 26 \\
450 & \text{if } w \geq 27
\end{cases}\)

For finer tuning, you could linearly interpolate between 340 and 450 from week 13 to 40, but that level of precision isn't typically necessary.

**Examples:**
- At 20 weeks (w=20): pregnancy_increment ≈ 340 kcal
- At 30 weeks (w=30): pregnancy_increment ≈ 400 kcal (midway between 340 and 450)
- At 36+ weeks: pregnancy_increment ≈ 450 kcal

The **simplified trimester-step values (0/340/450)** are usually sufficient according to health guidelines.

### Don't Forget Activity and Metabolism

Two women of the same size and week can have different needs if one is more active or has a higher metabolism. Always consider your lifestyle. If you're exercising regularly during pregnancy (which is generally encouraged in moderation), you might need a bit more fuel, especially on workout days. Conversely, if you became less active due to fatigue or medical reasons, your base caloric needs might be slightly lower.

### Monitor and Adjust

The best indicator that you're eating the right amount is appropriate weight gain and how you feel. If you follow the calculations and find you're consistently gaining too fast or too slow, adjust the calorie target by **100–200 kcal up or down** and see how it impacts your weight trend over a couple of weeks. Always keep your healthcare provider informed, especially if making significant changes.

## SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS AND COMMON CONDITIONS

Every pregnancy is unique. Certain common conditions or situations call for tailored nutritional attention.

### Gestational Diabetes (GDM)

If you develop gestational diabetes, your diet will need adjustments to control blood sugar. This typically means:

- Moderating carbohydrate portions
- Emphasizing complex carbs and fiber
- Avoiding spikes from sweets
- You might target carbs to ~40–45% of calories and avoid added sugars

**Tips:**
- Pair carbs with protein/fat to slow absorption (e.g., have peanut butter with an apple instead of candy)
- Eat smaller, more frequent meals
- Monitor blood sugar as directed by your healthcare provider

A registered dietitian or diabetes educator can help create a meal plan that keeps glucose levels in check while still providing needed nutrients for baby. Calorie needs in GDM are similar, but careful timing and composition of meals become crucial.

### Anemia (Iron or Folate Deficiency)

Pregnancy naturally lowers hemoglobin somewhat (due to blood volume expansion), but true anemia (low red blood cell count from nutrient deficiency) needs correction.

**Iron-deficiency anemia:**
- If your blood tests show low iron, ensure you get the 27 mg iron daily and follow medical advice on iron supplements (often 60–120 mg iron pills are given for anemia)
- Also eat iron-rich foods with vitamin C

**Folate-deficiency anemia:**
- Can occur if folic acid intake is insufficient
- The 600 µg folate from your prenatal and diet is the preventive measure
- Treating anemia early is important for preventing preterm delivery and low birthweight, as well as reducing your risk of extreme fatigue

### Vegetarian or Vegan Diets

Plant-based diets can support a healthy pregnancy, but pay attention to certain nutrients:

**Vitamin B12:**
- Not present in plant foods—must come from fortified foods or supplements
- Your prenatal likely has B12, but strict vegans should ensure a reliable B12 source of 2.6+ µg/day

**Iron and Zinc:**
- Found in beans, lentils, tofu, whole grains, nuts
- Plant forms are less readily absorbed
- You may need more than the standard 27 mg iron to keep levels up (your doctor will monitor)
- Pair with vitamin C foods to enhance absorption

**Calcium and Vitamin D:**
- Get from fortified plant milks, juices, tofu, almonds, leafy greens
- Consider a supplement if intake is low

**DHA Omega-3:**
- Consider an algal oil DHA supplement if you don't eat fish
- Ensure baby's brain development needs are met (200–300 mg DHA/day)

**Choline:**
- Focus on choline-rich plant foods like soy products, mushrooms, beans, quinoa
- Consider a supplement if needed to reach ~450 mg

**With good planning, a vegetarian or vegan can meet all needs, but it may require extra attention and possibly blood tests to check levels (B12, iron, vitamin D).**

### Nausea/Vomiting (Morning Sickness)

In early pregnancy, if you have significant nausea, eating enough can be challenging.

**Tips:**
- **Prioritize folic acid intake** (even if only via supplement and fortified cereal) during this critical time
- Eat whatever healthy foods you can tolerate in small portions (e.g., dry toast, crackers, bananas, yogurt, soup)
- Hydrate well
- As long as you're taking your prenatal vitamin, short-term low intake is usually okay
- If you can't keep fluids down or are losing weight, seek medical help (severe nausea may need medication or IV fluids)
- Most women feel better in second trimester and can "catch up" nutritionally
- When appetite returns, focus on protein and produce to make up for lost ground
- Vitamin B6 supplements (25–50 mg) are sometimes recommended for nausea relief—check with your doctor

### Heartburn

As pregnancy progresses, heartburn can limit how much you eat at one time. If this affects you:

- Opt for smaller, more frequent meals (5–6 mini-meals a day instead of 3 large ones)
- Avoid spicy or very fatty foods which can worsen reflux
- Ensure you still meet your nutrient needs by choosing nutrient-dense options each time you eat
- Don't lie down immediately after eating
- Consider propping your head up slightly when sleeping to reduce reflux

### High Blood Pressure/Preeclampsia

If you develop high blood pressure or are at risk for preeclampsia, diet considerations include:

- **Limit sodium:** Stick closer to 1,500–2,000 mg/day in such cases—follow your doctor's advice
- **Ensure adequate calcium:** Some studies show calcium supplementation (~1,000–1,500 mg) can help prevent high blood pressure in women with low dietary calcium
- **Focus on fruits and vegetables:** Provide potassium and magnesium that help regulate blood pressure
- **Stay hydrated**
- **Avoid excessive caffeine**

**Always report severe headaches, vision changes, or rapid swelling to your provider, as preeclampsia is a serious condition.**

### Multiple Pregnancies (Twins, Triplets)

Nutritional needs are higher when carrying more than one baby.

**Calories:**
- +600 kcal/day in second trimester
- +600–900 kcal in third trimester for twins/triplets

**Protein:**
- Aim for at least 25 g more protein per baby
- Twin pregnancy might need ~100+ g protein/day
- Consult specific guidelines with your healthcare provider

**Iron, calcium, and all vitamins:**
- Requirements are somewhat higher because two or more fetuses will draw on your nutrient stores
- Doctors often recommend mothers of multiples take higher-dose prenatal vitamins or additional supplements (e.g., extra folate, iron)

**Weight gain recommendations:**
- For twins with normal BMI: ~37–54 lbs

**Work closely with your healthcare team;** they may refer you to a nutritionist to ensure you're meeting the increased demands.

### Common Discomforts

Pregnancy can come with constipation, leg cramps, fatigue, etc. Interestingly, diet can help:

**Constipation:**
- High fiber and plenty of fluids help prevent constipation
- Include fruits, vegetables, whole grains, nuts

**Leg Cramps:**
- Adequate magnesium and calcium can sometimes alleviate leg cramps
- Include nuts, seeds, dairy, leafy greens

**From Iron Supplements:**
- Iron supplements can cause constipation
- Combat this with fiber and talk to your doctor if it's an issue
- They might switch you to a slow-release iron or add a stool softener

**Fatigue:**
- Ensure you're eating enough overall
- Check iron levels and treat if anemic
- Get adequate sleep and rest when possible

### Staying Active

Unless contraindicated, continue moderate exercise in pregnancy. It can improve energy, mood, and even help with digestion.

**Remember:**
- Fuel your activity—have a light snack with protein and carbs before workouts
- Stay hydrated
- Being active might allow for a bit more flexibility with calorie intake (since you're burning some)
- Don't exercise intensely to "diet"—the goal is to maintain fitness, not lose weight

## FOOD HANDLING & PREPARATION SAFETY TIPS

1. **Wash hands** frequently, especially before eating and after handling raw meat
2. **Separate raw meats** from ready-to-eat foods to prevent cross-contamination
3. **Use different cutting boards** for raw meats and other foods
4. **Clean utensils thoroughly** after contact with raw meat
5. **Refrigerate leftovers** within 2 hours (1 hour if hot weather)
6. **Consume leftovers within 3–4 days**
7. **Use food thermometer** to ensure proper cooking temperatures:
   - Meats: 165°F (74°C)
   - Seafood: 145°F (63°C)
   - Poultry: 165°F (74°C)
8. **Wash all produce** under running water before eating (or prepare it in ways where you can discard outer layers)
9. **Clean refrigerator regularly** to prevent bacterial growth
10. **Store fish and seafood** at proper cold temperatures
11. **Keep hot foods hot** and cold foods cold
12. **When in doubt about freshness**, throw it out

✓ Cook all meats, poultry, and seafood thoroughly to proper internal temperatures

✓ Ensure adequate iron, calcium, folate, and DHA
✓ Limit added sugars to 50 g per day maximum
✓ When in doubt, ask your healthcare provider

## PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

Pregnancy nutrition is about **quality and appropriate quantity**. To summarize the key points:

1. **Eat a balanced diet rich in:**
   - Protein (at least ~70 g/day)
   - Whole grains and carbohydrates (minimum 175 g carbs with high fiber)
   - Healthy fats (especially omega-3s)

2. **Ensure adequate vitamins and minerals daily,** particularly:
   - Folic acid
   - Iron
   - Calcium
   - Vitamin D
   - Iodine
   - Choline

3. **Through a combination of nutrient-dense foods and a prenatal supplement**

4. **Limit:**
   - Added sugars
   - Unhealthy fats
   - Sodium

5. **Avoid substances that could harm your baby:**
   - Alcohol (completely)
   - Excess caffeine (>200 mg/day)
   - Certain fish with mercury
   - Unpasteurized and raw foods

6. **By adjusting calorie intake based on your pre-pregnancy size and how far along you are, you can achieve the right amount of weight gain for a healthy pregnancy.**

Remember that these guidelines provide a starting point—it's normal to have variations in appetite and weight gain. **Stay in communication with your healthcare provider;** they will track your weight and may do blood tests to ensure you're on track nutritionally.

With mindful eating and a bit of planning, you can meet all the macro and micro nutrient needs for each week of your pregnancy, giving your baby the best start while also keeping yourself healthy and strong.
